Appearance:

Tigers are large, powerful felines with a distinctive orange coat and black stripes. Their fur can also be white with black stripes, known as the Bengal white tiger. They have muscular bodies, long legs, and a long, thick tail.

Size:

Tigers are the largest of the big cats. Males can weigh between 180 to 320 kg (400 to 700 lbs) and measure up to 3.3 meters (10.8 feet) from head to tail. Females are generally smaller.

Habitat:

Tigers are native to Asia and can be found in various habitats, including tropical forests, grasslands, and savannas. Historically, they roamed across much of Asia, but today their range has been significantly reduced.

Behavior:

Tigers are solitary animals and usually live alone, except for mothers with cubs or during mating. They mark their territory with scent markings and vocalizations.

Diet:

Tigers are carnivorous and primarily hunt large prey, such as deer, wild boar, and sometimes even young elephants or rhinos. They can eat up to 40 kg (88 lbs) of meat in a single meal.

Reproduction:

Tigers breed throughout the year, but most births occur in the winter. The gestation period is about 93 to 112 days, and a litter usually consists of 2 to 4 cubs.

Conservation Status:

Tigers are listed as endangered, with fewer than 4,000 individuals remaining in the wild. The primary threats to their survival include habitat loss and poaching.

Interesting Facts:

Each tiger has a unique pattern of stripes, much like human fingerprints. They are strong swimmers and can swim long distances to hunt or cool off.
